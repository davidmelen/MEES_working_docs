This Tier‑2 article supports UK landlords and advisors who need a practical read on when the Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards (MEES) apply to a domestic private rented property in England or Wales, and what to check first.

Disclaimer: This is general guidance based on Chapter 1 of the official landlord guidance. It is not legal advice. Complex or borderline scenarios should be escalated (see Tier‑3 guardrails below).

Quick compliance checks

  1. If the property is let on a relevant tenancy (assured/AST, regulated, or domestic agricultural), then it can be in scope of MEES; if not, it may be out of scope. [pp. 14–16]
  2. If the building is legally required to have an EPC, then MEES can apply; if no EPC is legally required (e.g., some protected buildings, certain HMOs, small stand‑alone buildings), MEES does not apply. [pp. 14, 16–21]
  3. If there is a valid EPC showing band E or above, the property meets the minimum standard—no MEES action is required. [p. 16]
  4. If there is a valid EPC showing F or G, the property is sub‑standard; improve to E or register a valid exemption. [p. 16]
  5. If granting, renewing, or extending a tenancy on/after 1 April 2018, you must not let an F or G unless a registered exemption applies. From 1 April 2020 you must not continue to let an in‑scope F or G without a registered exemption. [pp. 24–25]
  6. If subletting is involved and the tenant becomes a “new landlord”, that tenant must not sublet below E; superior landlords may need to consent to works. [pp. 26–27]
  7. If it’s a multi‑let/HMO or unit within a building, identify which EPC applies (unit vs whole building) and check whether an EPC is legally required. [pp. 19–20, 31–33]
  8. If listed or in a conservation area, there is no automatic exemption; if an EPC is required, the property is in scope—seek conservation advice where works would unacceptably alter character. [pp. 22–23, 34]
  9. If a voluntary EPC exists for a property that otherwise did not require one, MEES still doesn’t apply until you market the property for let (which triggers the legal EPC requirement). [p. 21]
  10. If you improve an F/G property, a fresh EPC is not legally required but is recommended to evidence compliance; keep records for enforcement. [pp. 18–19, 25]

Eligibility decision steps

  1. Confirm occupation type. Is it a tenancy (not a licence)? If yes, is it assured/AST, regulated, or domestic agricultural? If yes, proceed; otherwise likely out of scope. If unsure, seek legal advice. [pp. 14–16]
  2. Determine whether an EPC is legally required for this property or discrete unit. Check EPC exceptions (e.g., some protected buildings, certain HMOs, small stand‑alone buildings). [pp. 16–21]
  3. Locate the current EPC (EPC Register by address/reference) and confirm it is less than 10 years old. [pp. 18, 32]
  4. If the EPC is older than 10 years, you only need a new EPC when you next sell, market to let, or undertake certain modifications. Otherwise, the old EPC simply expires. [pp. 18–19]
  5. Check timing triggers. New, renewed, or extended tenancies from 1 Apr 2018 must meet E (or hold a registered exemption). From 1 Apr 2020, continuing lets must also meet E (or hold an exemption). [pp. 24–25]
  6. If EPC is F/G and in scope, either install relevant improvements to raise to E (note Chapter 1 cites a £3,500 inc. VAT cap contextually) or register a qualifying exemption before letting/continuing to let. [pp. 16, 23, 25]
  7. For HMOs/multi‑let, identify the relevant EPC (unit vs whole‑building) and re‑assess steps 3–6 accordingly. [pp. 19–20, 31–33]

Scope: properties & tenancies

MEES applies to domestic private rented properties in England and Wales that (a) are let on a relevant tenancy, and (b) are legally required to have an EPC. If both conditions are met, the rules apply regardless of building type, location, length of tenancy, listed status, or size. [p. 14]

Relevant tenancies are: assured (including AST) under the Housing Act 1988; regulated under the Rent Act 1977; and domestic agricultural tenancies defined in the 2015 Order. Social housing is excluded (see social housing carve‑outs in statute). [pp. 15–16]

Licence vs tenancy: licences are not tenancies for MEES. Where there is doubt about status or scope, the guidance advises seeking legal advice. [pp. 14–15]

EPC rules you must check

An EPC is normally required on construction, sale or letting, and must be available before marketing. EPCs are valid for 10 years; a new EPC is usually only required at the next sale/let or certain modifications. EPCs attach to the property, not the owner. [pp. 16–18]

A post‑improvement EPC is not legally required by MEES but is recommended to evidence that works achieved at least band E. Keep records if you rely on other evidence. [pp. 18–19, 25]

For multi‑let buildings there may be EPCs for individual units and/or the envelope. The relevant EPC is the one for the let space. If only the whole‑building EPC exists and a unit EPC is not legally required, use the whole‑building EPC. [pp. 19–20]

When an EPC is not required (examples): some listed/conservation buildings where compliance works would unacceptably alter character; places of worship; certain temporary buildings; small stand‑alone buildings (<50 m²); certain HMOs; furnished holiday accommodation; buildings to be demolished (strict conditions). Listed status is not an automatic MEES exemption—if an EPC is required, MEES applies. [pp. 20–21, 22–23]

Voluntary EPCs do not by themselves bring a property into MEES. Marketing that triggers a legal EPC requirement brings the property into scope (the voluntary EPC may then be used if current). [p. 21]

Prohibition dates & “new tenancy”

You must not grant a new tenancy, or renew/extend an existing tenancy, of an in‑scope property at EPC F or G from 1 April 2018 unless a valid exemption is registered. From 1 April 2020 you must not continue to let an in‑scope F/G property without a registered exemption. [pp. 24–25]

“Grant a new tenancy” occurs when a binding tenancy agreement is entered into (when terms are agreed), even if occupation begins later. A tenancy granted pre‑1 April 2018 but occupied after that date falls under the continuing‑let backstop by 1 April 2020 unless renewed/extended meanwhile. [p. 25]

Illustrative scenarios show that an expired EPC on a long fixed term may leave a continuing tenancy outside MEES until a new EPC is legally required (e.g., at remarketing or qualifying modification). Seek advice before relying on this. [pp. 23–24]

Subletting: who is responsible

If a tenant is allowed to sublet and becomes a new landlord to a sub‑tenant, they must not let below E unless an exemption is registered. They may need superior‑landlord consent to make improvements. [pp. 26–27]

From 1 April 2020, superior landlords are expected to have already brought properties to E (or registered exemptions) before downstream subletting occurs. Otherwise, subletting cannot lawfully proceed. [pp. 26–27]

Assignment is different from subletting; if no “new landlord” relationship is created, responsibility remains with the original landlord. When in doubt, take legal advice. [p. 27]

Mixed‑use & HHSRS interactions

In mixed‑use buildings (residential and commercial), determine whether the let space is a dwelling on a relevant tenancy (domestic MEES), or a non‑domestic letting (non‑domestic MEES). Triggers and duties differ by category. [pp. 28–29]

Separately, the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) lets councils act on hazards (e.g., excess cold, damp). A valid MEES exemption does not remove duties to remedy hazards; enforcement can still follow. [pp. 29–30]

The Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act (from 20 March 2019) requires landlords to keep properties free from serious hazards throughout a tenancy; tenants may seek court redress. [p. 30]

Escalation guardrails (Tier‑3 handoff)

  • Unclear whether the occupation is a licence or a tenancy (affects scope). [pp. 14–15]
  • Listed/conservation properties where EPC need and acceptable works are disputed. [pp. 22–23, 34]
  • Multi‑let/HMO with uncertainty about which EPC applies or if an EPC is required. [pp. 19–20, 31–33]
  • Long fixed‑term tenancies with expired EPCs and questions about the backstop/trigger logic. [pp. 23–25]
  • Subletting chains (new‑landlord liability, consent to improvements, assignment vs sublet). [pp. 26–27]
  • Mixed‑use buildings where domestic vs non‑domestic categorisation is unclear. [pp. 28–29]
  • Holiday lets or demolitions claimed as EPC‑exempt (strict tests). [pp. 20–21]

FAQ

When do the minimum standard provisions come into force?

MEES has two key triggers. From 1 April 2018, you must not grant, renew or extend a tenancy on an in‑scope property below EPC E unless a valid exemption is registered. From 1 April 2020, you must not continue to let an in‑scope F/G property without a registered exemption. These dates apply in England and Wales only. [pp. 24–25, 31]

Which tenancy types are covered?

Assured tenancies (including ASTs) under the Housing Act 1988, regulated tenancies under the Rent Act 1977, and domestic agricultural tenancies under the 2015 Order. Social housing is excluded. If your agreement is a licence, MEES does not apply. If unsure whether your agreement is a tenancy, seek legal advice. [pp. 15–16, 31]

What is an EPC and when is it required?

An Energy Performance Certificate rates a dwelling from A (best) to G (worst) and includes improvement recommendations. It is generally required when a property is constructed, or when marketed for sale or let, and must be available before marketing. It lasts 10 years and attaches to the property rather than the owner. [pp. 16–18, 32]

Are HMOs excluded from MEES?

No. MEES applies to any domestic PRS property that is legally required to have an EPC and is let on a relevant tenancy, including individual rooms. However, individual HMO rooms do not need their own EPC; the relevant EPC may be the whole‑building EPC if a unit EPC is not legally required. [pp. 18–20, 31–33]

Do listed buildings have to comply?

Listed/conservation buildings are not automatically exempt. If an EPC is legally required, the property is in scope and must meet E or hold a valid exemption. If EPC compliance would unacceptably alter the building’s character, an EPC may not be required; take conservation advice. [pp. 22–23, 34]

How long is an EPC valid and when would I need a new one?

EPCs last 10 years. You only need a new EPC when you next sell/market to let or after certain modifications. Obtaining a fresh EPC after improvement works is recommended to evidence compliance but is not mandatory under MEES. [pp. 18–19, 33]

Micro‑glossary (Chapter 1 terms)

Domestic private rented property (PRS)
A property in England or Wales that is let on a relevant domestic tenancy and is legally required to have an EPC; if both are true, MEES applies. [p. 14]
Relevant tenancy
Assured/AST (Housing Act 1988), regulated (Rent Act 1977), or domestic agricultural (2015 Order). Social housing is excluded. [pp. 15–16]
Sub‑standard property
A domestic PRS property with an EPC rating of F or G. [pp. 6–7]
Backstop date
From 1 April 2020, continuing lets of in‑scope F/G properties are prohibited unless a valid exemption is registered. [pp. 24–25]
HMO (House in Multiple Occupation)
A property with non‑self‑contained units/rooms let to multiple occupiers; individual rooms do not require their own EPC, but the property may need a whole‑building EPC. [pp. 18, 20, 31–33]
Mixed‑use property
Buildings containing both residential and commercial parts; determine if the let space is a dwelling (domestic MEES) or non‑domestic. [pp. 28–29]
