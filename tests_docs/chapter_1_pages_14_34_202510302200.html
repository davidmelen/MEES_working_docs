Chapter 1: How the Regulations Apply to Domestic Property

The Regulations mean that a domestic private rented property in England or Wales should not be let if its energy performance indicator is below EPC E. This chapter is aimed at domestic landlords and outlines the factors landlords need to consider when determining if a property they let is covered by the minimum level of energy efficiency provisions. Enforcement authorities and others with an interest in domestic rented property will also find this information useful.

1.1 Domestic private rented sector (PRS) scope

1.1.1 Properties covered by the minimum level of energy efficiency provisions

The Regulations apply to all domestic PRS properties in England and Wales which are:

  • let under certain types of domestic tenancy (see section 1.1.2), and
  • legally required to have an Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) (see section 1.1.4).

Where both conditions are met, the property is covered irrespective of type, tenancy length, location, listed status, or size. Where either condition is not met, the property is not covered.

Licence vs Tenancy

Licences are not the same as tenancies and do not count as tenancies for these Regulations. A tenancy grants exclusive possession; a licence is merely permission to do something on the property. Seek legal advice if unsure whether an arrangement is a licence or tenancy.

1.1.2 Relevant Tenancies (regulation 19)

For the purposes of the domestic minimum standard the relevant tenancy types are:

  • An assured tenancy (including an assured shorthold tenancy) defined in the Housing Act 1988;
  • A regulated tenancy defined in the Rent Act 1977;
  • A domestic agricultural tenancy as set out in the Energy Efficiency (Domestic Private Rented Property) Order 2015:
    • An assured agricultural occupancy (Housing Act 1988, s.24);
    • A protected occupancy (Rent (Agriculture) Act 1976, s.3(6));
    • A statutory tenancy (Rent (Agriculture) Act 1976, s.4(6)).

Social housing exclusion

The minimum standards do not apply to the social housing sector. A property let on a listed tenancy type is excluded if it is low cost rental or low cost home ownership accommodation under the Housing and Regeneration Act 2008 (sections 69–70), or where the landlord is registered as a social landlord.

1.1.3 Meeting the minimum standard, and sub-standard property (regulation 22)

A property meets the minimum standard if, from the trigger dates in section 1.2.1, it has a valid EPC showing a rating of E or above. A property is sub-standard (non-compliant) if a valid EPC shows F or G; in such cases the landlord must either improve to EPC E (see chapter two) or register an exemption (see chapter four).

1.1.4 Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) ratings

1.1.4.1 EPC Overview

The Regulations apply only to properties legally required to have an EPC (e.g., at sale, letting, construction, or qualifying modification) under relevant legislation. Not having a required EPC is unlawful and may result in penalties.

1.1.4.2 EPCs — Ten Year Validity

An EPC is valid for ten years. There is no automatic requirement to commission a new EPC when one expires unless a new trigger occurs (sale, letting to a new tenant, or qualifying modification). A post-improvement EPC is recommended to evidence compliance.

1.1.4.3 EPCs and multi-let buildings

Where multiple EPCs exist (e.g., whole building and discrete unit), the relevant EPC is the one for the “property” being let (building or part). Improvements required relate to the property being let.

1.1.4.4 Circumstances where an EPC may not be required

Examples include protected/listed buildings where compliance would unacceptably alter character, places of worship, certain temporary or small standalone buildings (<50m²), specified industrial/agricultural buildings with low energy demand, and some Houses in Multiple Occupation (HMOs) not previously sold/let as a single unit.

1.1.4.5 Voluntary EPCs where not legally required

A voluntary EPC does not by itself bring a property into scope. However, if the property is later marketed for let, the legal EPC requirement is triggered and the property then falls within scope (the voluntary EPC can be used if <10 years old).

Box Two — Listed Buildings and EPC Compliance

Listed buildings and those in conservation areas are not automatically exempt from EPCs; exemption applies only where meeting minimum energy performance requirements would unacceptably alter character or appearance. Landlords should determine applicability and may consult conservation officers.

Box Three — EPC Requirements, Ten Year Validity and the Minimum Energy Efficiency Standard

  • Scenario 1: New tenancy from 1 April 2019 — if EPC < 10 years old, use it; if F/G, improve to E (or register exemption) before issuing tenancy.
  • Scenario 2: 10-year assured tenancy with EPC F from 2015 — from 1 April 2020, continuing to let triggers compliance.
  • Scenario 3: 20-year tenancy with EPC F from 2009 — EPC expired in 2019; no new EPC required while tenancy continues, so property not captured until next trigger.

Note: EPC methodologies (e.g., RdSAP) are periodically updated and may alter banding.

1.2 When do the minimum level of energy efficiency provisions apply?

1.2.1 Prohibition on letting sub-standard domestic property (regulation 23)

Phased introduction: triggers for new, renewed, or extended tenancies from 1 April 2018; backstop date 1 April 2020 for all remaining tenancies. From these dates, landlords must not let or continue to let sub-standard (F/G) property unless an applicable exemption is registered.

Meaning of “grant a new tenancy”

A tenancy is granted when a binding tenancy agreement is entered into (terms finalised and intent to vest exclusive possession), even if possession starts later.

1.2.2 Subletting of domestic property

If a tenant is permitted to sublet and proposes to enter into a sub-tenancy, that tenant becomes a landlord and must comply (reach EPC E or register an exemption) before subletting. Superior landlord consent may be needed for improvements. From 1 April 2020, superior landlords should already have ensured compliance.

Box Four — Tenant obtaining landlord consent

Tenants seeking to improve energy efficiency may need landlord consent. If consent is unreasonably withheld, tenants may use the Tenants’ Energy Efficiency Improvements provisions (from April 2016) subject to securing suitable funding.

Example subletting scenarios

  • Pre–April 2020: Original tenant sublets in 2019 — compliance sits with the original tenant (now acting as landlord for the sub-tenancy).
  • Post–April 2020: Original tenant intends to sublet after backstop — superior landlord should already have improved to E or registered an exemption; otherwise subletting cannot lawfully proceed until rectified.

1.3 Mixed use properties and tenancy types

Where buildings include mixed residential and commercial units, the Regulations apply to rented units within such buildings; triggers depend on whether units are domestic or non-domestic. Determine the tenancy type to establish whether a property is domestic (and thus within these provisions) or non-domestic.

Box Five — The Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS)

Separate from energy efficiency, HHSRS (under the Housing Act 2004) assesses hazards such as damp, excess cold and can require remedial works. An exemption from EPC E does not remove obligations to address serious hazards.

Frequently Asked Questions Relevant to Chapter One

When did the minimum standard provisions come into force?
For new domestic tenancy agreements from 1 April 2018; for all tenancies from 1 April 2020 (unless a lawful exemption applies).
What tenancy types are covered?
Assured (including assured shorthold), regulated, and specified domestic agricultural tenancies.
Are these Regulations UK-wide?
No. They apply to England and Wales only.
When must properties be at EPC E?
All covered domestic PRS properties must be at EPC E (or have a valid exemption) by 1 April 2020. Between 1 April 2018 and 1 April 2020, compliance was required on entry into a new tenancy.
Are Houses in Multiple Occupation (HMOs) excluded?
HMOs are not excluded. Where legally required to have an EPC and let on a relevant tenancy, they are in scope (note: individual rooms usually do not require their own EPC).
